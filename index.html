Text, Tone, and Misreadings

Text, Tone, and Misreadings Text flattens people. It carries ideas, but it does not carry voice, pauses, or facial cues. What would sound calm and welcoming in speech can appear sharp once written, even when the intent is unchanged. This is clearer in technical discussions. Clear conclusions and direct reasoning often read louder between letters than they do in real conversation. Certainty, in text, is easily mistaken for harshness, arrogance, being a "know-it-all". That is not always a matter of character; often it is a property of the medium itself. People also differ in how they communicate. Some prefer a gradual, softened approach. Others state the conclusion plainly and then walk through the reasoning. These are methods, and they reflect style, not the state of a person's heart. It helps to keep things from getting mixed, to realize that: * An argument is weighed by its evidence. * Tone shapes how that argument is received in a given context. * Intentions, however, are not visible to others. Those belong to a different domain altogether, and they are known only to Allāh. So when something written feels firm, understand that firmness is directed at ideas, not at people. And when words come across sharper than intended, that is often a limitation of text, not a reflection of character. Allāh knows best.

What Is an Image? Original vs Copied

What is an image? By "image" we mean the sensory, visible image, which is of two types: 1. Original image: These are the images that Allaah created in their original form. An image that can only be perceived by seeing the light emitted after it reflects off the created being. 2. Copied image: This is the main point of discussion—the image whose creation is forbidden if it represents that of an animate being. It is: Any visual representation of something that allows you to perceive how the thing looks without relying on the thing itself or its existence. In other words: Any visual representation of something that is perceived due to light reflected from something other than the thing itself. So, whenever you perceive how something looks without looking directly at it or its reflection, know that you are looking at a copied image of it, even if it perfectly matches it in every detail. This image is not light emitted or reflected from the thing itself, but rather from light emitted or reflected from something separate from its being, having an independent existence in itself, created through a process, using a machine, and stored in a material. How Does a Digital Camera Work?

How does a digital camera work? A modern digital camera contains millions of tiny light sensors, so small that they cannot be seen with the naked eye. Each light sensor captures the intensity and color of light and converts that light into electrical signals. The computer inside the camera then processes these signals and adjusts color information such as brightness, luminance, and their position within the captured frame, based on the pattern detected by the light sensors. This allows the camera to preserve high-resolution detail. The greater the number of sensors, the higher the resolution. The data is stored in the camera’s memory in the form of digital information, so that it can be used later to generate an unlimited number of images. You could say that the data is like a recipe that enables you to cook the same dish over and over again. Source: https://arm-alqaddari.codeberg.page/books/buzoogh/

Why Mirrors Reflect vs Cameras Create

Why do we see the reflection of objects on polished surfaces even though the objects are not actually in them? Shiny surfaces such as mirrors, glass, or water are smooth, which allows light to reflect in an orderly manner, because it encounters no significant resistance that would prevent it from leaving the surface. This illusory perception occurs through the following process: 1. Reflection of light: Light rays from the object reach the mirror’s surface and are reflected at an angle equal to the angle at which they hit the surface, in accordance with the law of reflection. 2. Light reaching your eyes: The reflected rays travel toward your eyes. When the brain processes this reflected light, it interprets the light as if it were coming from the mirror, since that is the last point from which the light appears to originate, meaning the line between it and the eye is straight. 3. Perception of the image: Your brain interprets these reflected rays as an object located behind the mirror, even though the actual object exists in front of it and outside it. Four Responses: Mirror vs Camera

If someone were to say: “Then apply the same rule to a person standing facing a mirror. Did he not perform an action, namely turning toward the mirror and standing in front of it, after which his image appeared in it?” He shall get a response from four different angles: 1. By rejecting the premise: What is in the mirror is not an image, but a reflection of an image. That is, there is no re-creation, only a redirection of light. Instead of light rays traveling directly from the object to your eye, they take a different path to reach your eye, and vision occurs. Thus, what truly happens is a reflection of an image, or more precisely, a reflection of the light rays reflected from the object. For this reason, what you see in the mirror cannot be retained, and it disappears when the position of the object changes. To bring this concept closer, try the following: Approach a wall, then direct a mirror toward it, and shine a light onto the mirror. You will notice a bright spot on the wall, and that is the reflected light rays. If you turn off the light, the spot disappears, confirming the concept of pure reflection without any image-making. Moreover, confining light in a way that allows us to see it is scientifically and self-evidently impossible. If, in addition, one were to arrange it in the same pattern and intensity with which it reflected from objects so that we could see them, then that's even more impossible. We see light only because it reaches our eyes. So how can it be claimed that it was confined and yet still seen? This contradiction is obvious. 2. By concession: If we were to concede that what is in the mirror is an image that has been produced, then it is distinguished from other images by the fact that the Messenger, salla-llaahu 'alayhi wa sallam, used it. Thus, the dispute returns to whether photography is merely reflection like a mirror, or a precise re-making of an image by means of a machine. We have already clarified the falseness of the mirror analogy, inshaa'Allaah, and to Him belong al-hamd and shukr, and He is al-Musta'aan. 3. By concession, then rejection: If we were to concede that what is in the mirror is an image that has been produced, then who produced it? The person standing in front of the mirror? How? By merely standing there? If that were the case, there would have been no need to invent cameras. What, then, is the difference? Fixation? What you call fixation is what we call image-making. So what then? The dispute once again returns to whether photography is like a mirror, or a precise re-making of an image by means of a machine. That is the core of the issue. The reason returns to perception and cognition occurring within the brain. Since the mirror’s surface is the last point from which light travels in a straight line toward the eye, the image appears to you there, but it is not truly there. Everything we see must lie along a straight line in front of our eyes, and for this reason a shadow forms when you place an opaque object in front of a light source. Further confirmation of this is that we see things behind the mirror as though it were a world on the other side, while it is known that this is not the case. Digital Camera: Eye, Brain, Hand, Canvas

Note: The following might be too technical to some, so if you don't understand it, that's okay, you don't have to. The Digital Camera Is an Eye, a Brain, a Hand, Paint, and Canvas How so? • The eye: The camera lens is designed similar to the lens of the human eye, which regulates the amount and angle of incoming light. Just as a painter using a brush must open his eyes, a camera must as well allow light to enter through its lens. Then: The retina of the eye converts light into electrical neural signals, which are then transmitted to the brain. Likewise, the light sensors in a camera convert light into electrical signals that are transmitted to its internal computer—its “brain”. • The brain: The brain processes these data and enables us to perceive and comprehend what the eye has captured. There is no copied image; rather, we merely apprehend what is around us—and this is a significant distinction. In addition, our brains may store this information, or they may not. The camera likewise processes these data, but it stores them simply as digital data in memory, so that it may produce images based on them, as follows: • The hand: When the photographer wishes to display the captured image on the screen (in devices that do not display the captured image automatically), the computer generates a copy of the digital data from memory and processes it again—but this time in reverse. It produces electrical signals using the device’s battery, at levels determined by the digital data. These electrical signals are transmitted to the screen, which consists of an enormous number of tiny lamps. Each tiny lamp illuminates according to the electrical signal it receives, adopting the color and brightness level it is commanded to display. After that, all of this complexity is essentially what the brush-using painter does as well: his brain moves his hand— to dip the brush into paint of the appropriate color, then to place the colored brush in its proper locations on the canvas. The difference lies in precision and speed: neither the brush nor the hand can achieve the precision and speed of a camera. • The paint: Both the brush-using painter and the camera use light; however, the painter uses paint to recreate the scene, whereas the camera uses the light of the screen’s lamps—which were previously electricity, and before that, stored digital data. • The canvas: The painter coats the canvas with paint, while the camera “coats” the screen with tiny lights. The canvas cannot be reused unless it is washable, whereas the screen is used repeatedly until it breaks or becomes defective. Their goal is one: to display the details of the reproduced image. Therefore, the camera resembles a tool that combines the functions of the eye, the brain, the hand, the paint, and the canvas within a small, closed system—whose purpose is… image-making. Commentary – Authentic Guidance Requires Erasing Images

This narration, which is the strongest in al-Musnad about that event, reports that Jibrīl (ʿalayhi as‑salām) said: “Remove the heads from the curtain and convert it into a carpet or cushions to be stepped upon”. The authentic guidance requires either removing the heads or doing both removal and treading — stepping on them alone is an unauthenticated addition. This occurred despite his long absence, her being the most beloved person to him, and using the curtain to decorate the house in welcome. This demonstrates clearly that tasweer is harām, a grave major sin, that provokes anger in those who truly understand that, and has an excellent role model in the Prophet, ṣallallāhu ʿalayhi wa-sallam. Al-Albani – Obliterating the Face Only Is Not Enough (Text)

Obliterating The Face Only is Not Enough — Al-Albānī السائل: حديث "الصورة الرأس، فإذا قطع الرأس فلا صورة". [هل] هذا يدخل في طمس الوجه إذا توفر هناك صورة أو تمثال لو افترضنا في البيت، هل يجوز طمس الوجه وهذا يكفي؟ Questioner: The ḥadīth states: "The image is the head, so if the head is cut off, there is no image". Does this include obliterating the face? If there happens to be an image or a statue, for example, in the house, is it permissible to obliterate the face [only], and would that be sufficient? الألباني: ليس الوجه، [بل] الرأس. لأن قولك "الوجه" يعني بقاء الرأس وطمس الوجه [فقط]. Al-Albānī: Not the face, rather the head. Because when you say "the face [only]", that implies the head remains while only the face is obliterated. والحديث يقول "الصورة الرأس"، أي إذا قطع الرأس وبقي بقية الجسم صار كهيئة الشجرة كما جاء في الحديث الصحيح. Yet the ḥadīth says: "The image is the head". Meaning that if the head is removed while the rest of the body remains, it becomes like the form of a tree, as has come in the authentic ḥadīth. فلابد من الاستغناء عن رأس الصورة كُلًا. Therefore, the head of the image must be removed in its entirety. ——— Translated by: ʿAbdurraḥmān bnu Mīhūb al-Qaddārī al-Jazāʾirī ——— 82 137 202 220

Note Regarding Partial and Faceless Heads

بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم السلام عليكم ورحمة الله وبركاته A note regarding partial and faceless heads: The argument that cutting off the head means merely wiping away the facial features is weak, very weak. Basic language does not allow such reasoning. Cutting is not wiping. Removing the head is not the same as keeping the head-shape and disturbing the face. The narration explicitly mentions cutting off the head, not blurring it, not hiding the eyes, not removing half of it, and not leaving enough of it for the image to remain what it was. Even the texts that mention طمس must be understood through the explicit texts that explain how such طمس is to be done. Do not leave the explicit wording, then interpret the general text however you see fit, whether the one doing that is a scholar or otherwise. Some scholars do hold this weak view, because scholars can be mistaken. A mistaken explanation does not override the explicit wording of the evidence. So no, we are not treating this as an equally strong position. At the same time, not every weak argument needs to become a war. Some people will not be convinced. Some will keep arguing. Some will stack quotations and linguistic claims until the discussion becomes fog. In such cases, the lesser evil may be to leave the argument and avoid conflict. But leaving the argument does not mean accepting the practice. We have every right to keep our own spaces free from partial heads, blurred faces, cropped heads, AI remnants, and similar methods. Especially because people do not copy the academic discussion behind the image. They copy what they see. They see a little left, then leave more. They see one exception, then make ten. They see the fence bent, then walk through it. This is how boundaries die: not usually by one dramatic attack, but by repeated small permissions. عياذا بالله. So be patient with people. Do not make every case a battlefield. But do not let weak arguments set the rule in your own space. Patience is one thing. Handing over the keys is another. والله أعلم. أخوكم أبو سهل، عبد الرحمن القداري.
